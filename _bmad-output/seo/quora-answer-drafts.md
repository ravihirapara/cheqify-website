## Answer Draft 1 — "How do I print a cheque in India?"

> *Word count: ~340. Maps to posts #4 + #7. Use for "how to print cheque at home" variations too.*

```
Printing your own cheques in India is completely legal and most major banks expect it now under CTS-2010. The mechanics break into three parts: the cheque leaf, the software, and the printer.

The cheque leaf — you have to use your own bank's issued cheque book. You can't print on plain paper. Each leaf already has your MICR band, account number, and the CTS-2010 security features pre-printed. What you're printing is just the variable data: date, payee, amount in figures, amount in words, and your signature space.

The software — most Indian SMBs use one of three approaches:

1. Excel templates with hand-aligned coordinates for each bank. Cheap but fragile. The alignment breaks every time you change printer trays, and you'll spend hours fixing it.
2. Paid desktop software like MoneyFlex or Chrysanth Cheque Writer. Works fine but locks you into Windows, costs ₹3,000+ per year, and the bank-format library is limited.
3. Browser-based tools that have Indian bank layouts pre-loaded. The newest of these is what most accountants I know have moved to in the last 2 years.

The printer — any inkjet or laser printer works. Make sure the paper feed is set to "manual" or "tray 2" so the cheque doesn't crumple on entry. Use only blue or black ink — red, pencil, and gel-metallic inks get the cheque rejected at the bank counter.

A few rules everyone forgets:
- Don't print over the MICR band at the bottom.
- Test on a photocopy of the cheque first, never the original leaf.
- Use the same software for every cheque — your signature position must stay consistent.

For a step-by-step walkthrough of the modern browser-based way, including the 300+ bank layouts most Indian SMBs need, see this guide: https://cheqify.app/en/blog/how-to-print-cheque-in-india

## Answer Draft 2 — "How long does a cheque take to clear in India?"

> *Word count: ~310. Maps to post #17.*

```
T+1 is the official answer the RBI quotes, but the actual answer depends on three things: when you deposited it, where it was drawn, and whether anything on the cheque is unusual.

The cleanest case: you deposit a local CTS-2010 cheque before your bank's cut-off (usually 11 AM to 1 PM depending on the branch) on a working day. The cheque clears T+1 — money in your account by next morning. This is what RBI markets.

The actual variations:

1. Outstation cheques used to take 5–7 days. Since the national CTS grid consolidated in April 2021, "outstation" is functionally gone — a Mumbai-deposited Bengaluru cheque takes the same T+1 as a same-city one.
2. Deposit after the cut-off, and the cheque is treated as if you deposited the next working day. So a Friday-evening deposit = Monday-morning capture = Tuesday credit.
3. Holidays add days. RBI clearing runs on weekdays and 2nd/4th Saturdays. So a cheque deposited Friday before a long weekend doesn't credit till Tuesday.
4. Bounced cheques get a return memo within 24–48 hours of capture with a reason code. Your bank reverses the provisional credit if it was given.
5. Signature mismatch, amount-word mismatch, or any drawing irregularity adds delay. About 1.5–3% of cheques get returned for these reasons.

The clearing infrastructure behind this is the Cheque Truncation System (CTS) — your physical cheque doesn't actually move between banks anymore. A high-resolution image and the MICR data travel through RBI's banking network, and the paper original stays at your branch in a sealed pouch.

If you want the full step-by-step of what happens between deposit and credit, this explainer breaks it down: https://cheqify.app/en/blog/cheque-clearing-time-india

— Ravi

## Answer Draft 3 — "What is the difference between a crossed cheque and an account payee cheque?"

> *Word count: ~330. Maps to post #23.*

```
This is one of the most-confused questions in Indian banking, so the short answer first: account payee is a kind of crossing. They're not separate things — every "account payee cheque" is also a crossed cheque, but not every crossed cheque is account payee.

Crossing is the legal term for the two parallel diagonal lines you draw in the top-left corner of a cheque. The Negotiable Instruments Act (Sections 123–131) defines four types:

1. General crossing — just two lines, nothing between them. Says "pay through any bank account, not cash over counter". Was the default before the 1990s; rare now.

2. Special crossing — two lines with a specific bank's name written between them (e.g., "HDFC Bank"). Cheque can only be cleared through that named bank. Used in a few government and legal-fee workflows; otherwise rare.

3. Account payee crossing — two lines with "A/c Payee Only" or "Account Payee" written between them. The cheque can only be credited to the named payee's bank account. Cannot be endorsed to a third party. This is the modern default for almost every Indian business cheque.

4. Not negotiable — two lines with "Not Negotiable" written between them. Sometimes combined with A/c Payee Only. Limits the "holder in due course" protection that normally shields recipients of stolen cheques. Adds belt-and-braces protection on top of account-payee.

In practice, "crossed cheque" colloquially means "account payee cheque" because that's the only crossing 99% of Indian businesses use in 2026. But legally they're different categories.

If you skip the crossing entirely, the cheque is a bearer cheque — anyone holding it can encash it at the counter. Loss = lost money. Cross every cheque you don't personally hand to the named payee at the bank.

The full deep-dive with NI Act references is here: https://cheqify.app/en/blog/crossed-cheque-vs-account-payee

— Ravi

## Answer Draft 4 — "Why does my cheque get returned / bounced?"

> *Word count: ~320. Maps to post #2 + #11.*

```
Cheque returns split into two buckets — funds-related and drawing-related — and the legal consequences differ. Knowing which bucket your return falls into determines what to do next.

Funds-related returns (Reason Codes 11–15 on the RBI matrix):
- Insufficient funds
- Exceeds arrangement
- Effects not cleared (the depositor used incoming funds that haven't actually cleared yet)

These are the legally serious returns. Under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act, an "insufficient funds" bounce can lead to a criminal complaint by the payee. The payee has 30 days from the return memo to issue a legal demand notice; if you don't pay within 15 days of the notice, they can file a Section 138 case. Penalties go up to twice the cheque amount and/or 2 years' imprisonment.

Drawing-related returns (the more common kind):
- Signature mismatch — your signature doesn't match the bank specimen on file
- Amount in words and figures differ
- Overwriting on any field
- Stale (>3 months old) or post-dated (presented before the date written)
- Crossing irregularity or missing endorsement
- Cheque torn, smudged, or MICR band damaged

Drawing returns don't trigger Section 138. They're typically resolved by reissuing the cheque correctly. But they still cost your business: bank processing fee (₹100–500), the lost time, and the reputational dent with the payee.

The single biggest cause across both buckets is signature mismatch — about 1.5–3% of all Indian cheques get returned for it. If your signature has evolved over years and the bank still has a 2015 specimen, you'll keep getting returns. Update the specimen at the branch — 10 minutes at SBI, HDFC, or ICICI.

A practical reasons-and-solutions breakdown for each return code is here: https://cheqify.app/en/blog/cheque-bounce-reasons-and-solutions

— Ravi

## Answer Draft 6 — "What is Section 138 of NI Act?" / "What happens if a cheque bounces in court?"

> *Word count: ~310. Maps to post #11.*

```
Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act 1881 is the criminal provision that lets a cheque payee take a cheque issuer to court when the cheque bounces for "insufficient funds" or "exceeds arrangement" — not for any other return reason.

The standard sequence of events:

1. Cheque is deposited. Bank returns it with a memo citing "insufficient funds" (or "exceeds arrangement").
2. The payee has 30 days from the return memo to issue a legal demand notice to the cheque issuer. The notice must specify the amount and demand payment within 15 days.
3. If the issuer pays within 15 days of receiving the notice — case closed.
4. If the issuer doesn't pay, the payee can file a Section 138 complaint at the magistrate's court within the next 30 days.
5. The case is heard. Penalties go up to twice the cheque amount and/or 2 years' imprisonment, plus compensation to the payee.

Several practical points most people miss:

- The complaint must be filed within strict timelines. Miss the 30-day notice window or the 30-day filing window after that, and the case is statute-barred.
- The cheque must have been "in discharge of debt or liability". A cheque you gifted, or one issued as security for an unfinalised agreement, may not qualify.
- The signature on the cheque must be authentic. If the issuer can prove forgery, Section 138 doesn't apply (the matter shifts to fraud under other sections).
- The case is criminal, not civil. The court can impose imprisonment, but the civil recovery of the amount usually runs separately under Order 37 of CPC.

A full explainer of how Section 138 works in practice, including the recent procedural changes from the 2018 amendment, is here: https://cheqify.app/en/blog/section-138-ni-act-cheque-bounce-india

This is general information, not legal advice — for any actual case, consult a practising advocate.

— Ravi

## Answer Draft 7 — "What is MICR code on a cheque?"

> *Word count: ~280. Maps to post #6.*

```
MICR stands for Magnetic Ink Character Recognition. The MICR codeline is the row of slightly weird-looking numbers and symbols at the very bottom of every Indian cheque — printed in magnetic ink so it can be read by bank scanners even if the rest of the cheque is faded, smudged, or partially obscured.

The MICR codeline encodes three things, in order, separated by the small symbols:

1. Cheque number (6 digits, first segment) — uniquely identifies this leaf within your cheque book.
2. MICR code (9 digits, second segment) — encodes your bank branch's city, bank, and branch code. The first 3 digits = city; next 3 = bank; last 3 = branch.
3. Account number (variable digits, third segment) — your account at that branch.

The whole point of MICR is that during the CTS clearing process, the scanner's magnetic head reads this strip in milliseconds and uses it to route the cheque image to the correct drawee bank. Without a clean MICR, the cheque has to be manually rerouted — adds delay, sometimes results in a return.

Practical rules:

1. Never write over the MICR band. Even a single pen stroke disrupts the magnetic ink.
2. Don't fold a cheque through the MICR. Folds crack the magnetic ink and the scanner mis-reads.
3. Don't photocopy and print on the photocopy — photocopier toner is not magnetic ink. The cheque will be rejected at first scan.
4. If your bank ever changes your branch's MICR code (rare but happens with mergers), your old cheque book becomes invalid. Surrender it and issue a new one.

A more detailed explainer with examples from real cheque leaves is here: https://cheqify.app/en/blog/what-is-micr-code-on-cheque

— Ravi
